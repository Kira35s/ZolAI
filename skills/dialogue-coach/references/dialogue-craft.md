# Dialogue craft checklist — reference for dialogue-coach

A working checklist with examples. None of these are absolute rules — a character
who *is* defined by stating things on the nose is a choice, not an error — but
each pattern below is worth a deliberate decision rather than an accident.

## Voice differentiation (the priority check)

Read the scene with names and tags covered. If two characters' lines could be
swapped without the reader noticing, the bible's voice profile isn't making it
onto the page. Common causes: both characters use the same sentence length and
vocabulary register, neither has a verbal tic or avoidance pattern, or both
default to direct statements with no character-specific filter.

> Flat: "I don't think we should go." / "I agree, it's too risky."
> Differentiated: "I don't think we should go." / "Risky. We're not doing it."

## On-the-nose dialogue

Characters say exactly what they think and feel, with no gap between interior
state and speech. Real speech (and good fictional speech) deflects, hedges, or
talks around the actual subject.

> On-the-nose: "I'm furious that you lied to me."
> With subtext: "Funny how you remembered everything *except* that."

## Said-bookisms and adverb crutches

Leaning on the dialogue tag (or an adverb bolted onto "said") to do the emotional
work that the line itself should be doing.

> Crutch: "Get out," she said angrily.
> Carried by the line: "Get out. Now."

## Info-dumping

Exposition delivered as dialogue between people who would already know it,
purely for the reader's benefit ("As you know, Bob, the reactor's been unstable
since the merger...").  If both speakers already know it, find another way to
deliver the information, or have someone genuinely ignorant ask.

## Uniform rhythm

Every line in the scene runs the same length and cadence regardless of who's
talking or what's at stake. Tension usually compresses sentences; comfort or
rambling expands them — if every exchange reads the same tempo, it reads flat
no matter the content.

## Dead air

Lines that don't escalate, reveal, or shift the subtext — they're technically
dialogue but the scene would lose nothing if they were cut. A useful test: does
this line change what either character wants, knows, or risks? If not, it's a
candidate to cut or compress.
